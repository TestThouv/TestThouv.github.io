NOUNS

Nouns are words that name persons, places, thoughts, or just a thing. Concrete nouns name things that can be heard, touched, or seen, like persons or places. Abstract nouns are nouns that name things that are imaginary. You can't see, hear, or touch them. Thoughts and ideas are good examples.

Common nouns name any generic thing, person, or place. They don't get capitalized. Proper nouns name a specific person, place, or event. They Get Capitalized.

Examples of nouns include: teacher, Faher, physicist, Einstein.

VERBS

There are two kinds of verbs: action verbs and linking verbs. Action verbs tell the reader what the subjec is doing. Linking verbs "link" a subject to a noun or adjective. Helping verbs are linking verbs that connect the subject to a present participle.

Examples of action verbs include (to) cook and (to) examine. Some examples of linking verbs are: (to) be, (to) become, (to) feel. Some helping verbs example in sentences are: "Martin felt oppressed." and "Just like every other night, Kristina was starting to prepare dinner.".

Some examples of verbs happening in this image are: cook and stir.

ADJECTIVES

Adjectives are words that describe nouns or prounous. You know that a word is an adjective if you can put "He is" or "She is" in front of it.

There are three forms of adjectives: positive (normal), comparative (used to compare two nouns or pronouns), and superlative (to express a comparison of three or more adjectives, to the highest degree).

Some examples of adjectives could be: ripe, riper, ripest, bad, worse, worst, explicit, more explicit, and most explicit.

The shape is more rectangular than a circle, striped, and blue.

ADVERBS

Adverbs are words used to describe verbs, adjectives, or other adverbs. Some can also modify the meaning of a verb or adjective.

Most adverbs are formed by taking the adective and adding -ly at the end. However, some, like soon aren't related in any way to an adjective.

Some adverbs are: promptly, exquisitly, and tomorrow.

The turtle is running slowly.

PRONOUNS

Pronouns are words used to replace a noun or nominal group and make the sentence simpler.

There are a lot of different kinds of pronouns, some of the most common ones being: personal pronouns (I, she, we, etc...), possessive pronouns (your, his, their, etc...), and reflexive pronouns (myself, yourself, themselves, itself, etc...)

Some examples of pronouns include: I, their, ourselves.

The nominal group Bob the boy is interchangeable with the pronoun he.

PREPOSITIONS

Prepositions are short words that show how words, groups of words, or ideas are related to each other.

There are dozens and dozens of prepositions, but some common examples are: to, in, at, and towards.

The people are going in Chipotle to eat a meal based out of Mexican food.

CONJUNCTIONS

Conjunctions connect words, groups of words, sentences, and even paragraphs.

Some examples of conjunctions are because, but, and whether.

I don't know whether I should get the first or second salad... The first one has cilantro, so I will take the second one.

INTERJECTIONS

Interjections are used to express an exclamation, such as Oh! or Oops!.

However, they can also be used as questions, such as What?, because, even though "What?" is grammatically a question, it shows the person is exclaming themselves, hence it is an interjection.

Common examples of interjections include Oh, dear!, Oh my gosh!, and Oops!.
